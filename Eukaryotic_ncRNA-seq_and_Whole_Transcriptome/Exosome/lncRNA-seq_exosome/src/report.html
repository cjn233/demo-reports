1 Introduction

1.1 Exosome concept and function

1.1.1 Exosome concept

Exosomes are extracellular vesicle structures that are produced from cytoplasm and released by cell membrane, with a diameter between 30 and 150nm, and carry a variety of proteins, lipids and nucleic acids of the mother cell (Kalluri R et al., 2020). Exosomes are abundant in sources, not only in plants, animals and microorganisms, but also in special liquids such as milk and beer. For example, exosomes are widely distributed in blood, saliva, urine, cerebrospinal fluid, milk and other body fluids, and almost all living cells may secrete exosomes. Therefore, the background signal of exosome research is mixed, and individual differences are relatively large.

1.1.2 Exosome function

Exosomes, with their heterogeneity and rich markers, can be associated with almost any disease and become a research hotspot in the medical field. Together with ctDNA and CTC, exosomes are called the "troika" of liquid biopsy. With the deepening of exosome research, its importance in human life activities has become increasingly prominent.

1.2 Function and research significance of exosome lncRNA

Different from ordinary tissues and cells, the RNA fragments in exosomes are relatively small, mainly concentrated in 25-200nt. Lncrnas are generally in the range of 200-2000nt, and the fragments are relatively short. In addition, smallRNA accounted for a relatively high proportion of exosomes (Yuan T et al., 2016). Exosome lncRNA plays an important regulatory role in regulating physiological and biochemical reactions such as ontogeny, cell proliferation and differentiation, occurrence, development, metastasis and invasion of tumors (diseases) (Yousefi H et al., 2019), and regulates gene expression from three levels: 1) Epigenetic regulation, lncrnas can recruit chromatin remodeling complexes to specific sites and then mediate the expression silencing of related genes; 2) Transcriptional regulation: lncrnas can mediate gene silencing at the transcriptional level and also interfere with the expression of neighboring genes; 3) Post-transcriptional regulation, lncRNA can regulate gene expression by forming double chains with mRNA. Studies on lncrnas in exosomes can deeply understand the internal mechanism and functional regulation mechanism of diseases or different developmental stages, and screening exosome specific biomarkers also has a very high clinical application value (Cheng J et al., 2020; Szabo G et al., 2017).

2.2 Library Construction, Quality Control and Sequencing

Total RNA was used as input material for the RNA sample preparations. Sequencing libraries were generated using RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina following manufacturer’s recommendations and index codes were added to attribute sequences to each sample. Briefly, mRNA was purified from total RNA using probes to remove rRNA. Fragmentation was carried out using divalent cations under elevated temperature in First Strand Synthesis Reaction Buffer(5X). First strand cDNA was synthesized using random hexamer primer and M-MuLV Reverse Transcriptase (RNaseH). Second strand cDNA synthesis was subsequently performed using DNA Polymerase I and RNase H. Remaining overhangs were converted into blunt ends via exonuclease/polymerase activities. After adenylation of 3’ ends of DNA fragments, Adaptor with hairpin loop structure were ligated to prepare for hybridization. In order to select cDNA fragments of preferentially 370~420 bp in length, the library fragments were purified with AMPure XP system (Beverly, USA). Then 3 µL USER Enzyme was used with size-selected, adaptor-ligated cDNA at 37°C for 15 min followed by 5 min at 95 °C before PCR. Then PCR was performed with Phusion High-Fidelity DNA polymerase, Universal PCR primers and Index (X) Primer. At last, PCR products were purified (AMPure XP system) library quality was assessed on the Agilent 5400 system（Agilent，USA） and quantified by QPCR (1.5 nM).

The Qualified libraries were pooled and sequenced on Illumina platforms with PE150 strategy, according to effective library concentration and data amount required.

Sequencing error rate is related to the base quality and is affected by many factors such as the sequencer, the sequencing reagents and the samples. For RNA-Seq technology, the distribution of sequencing error rate has two features:

(1) The error rate will increase when the length of sequenced reads increases. This is due to the consumption of chemical reagents during sequencing, which is common for all Illumina high-throughput sequencing platforms(Hardwick SA et al., 2016).

(2) The first six bases will suffer from higher sequencing error rate, and this is also the length of random primers which are required for reverse transcription during RNA-seq library construction. Therefore, it is speculated that the higher error rate in the first six bases is due to the incomplete bindings between random primers and RNA templates (Hardwick SA et al., 2016).

Q:The error rate will increase with the higher length of sequencing, what is the acceptable range of error rate?
A: Novogene set a high standard of sequencing quality. Generally, the error rate of a single base should be lower than 1%. In some special cases, the maximum error rate of a single base should not be greater than 6%.
Q:What is the difference between the align software of RNA data and of DNA data?
A:Due to the existence of splicing during transcription, a large portion of the measured reads spans different exons, and all RNA data alignment software will support the splice alignment of spliced reads.
Q:Does the full length of reads will be used for mapping, or does the head or tail of reads will be processed before mapping?
A:In our experiment, we use the standard RNA-seq kit, whose indexes are in the middle of the adapters and the sequencing will be executed for the sequences with indexes. In this way, the sequences of read 1 and read 2 are from the samples and there is no need to process the sequences in mapping. Even if the adapters are in sequences or the quality is low, the read will be removed.

4.3.2 Transcript Identification

We use the Stringtie software merge parameter to merge the transcripts obtained from the assemblied samples, and remove the ones with uncertain chain direction and the length of the transcripts not exceeding 200nt, then we use gffcompare software to compare with the known database, filter out the known transcripts in the database, and finally predict the coding potential of the new transcripts, and finally get Novel_lncRNA and Novel_mRNA.

We process the file by a series of filter steps:

Step1: Exon Number Filter: Filter out transcrips with low expression level and single exon transcripts with low confidence. Select transcripts with exon> = 2 (Single exon transcripts will be kept for plant data);
Step2: Transcript Length Filter: Select transcripts with length> 200bp;
Step3: Known Transcript Filter: Filter out transcripts which overlap with annotated exon region in database using gffcompare. Add these annotations to lncRNA analysis;
Step4: Encoding potential screening: whether there is encoding potential is the key to determine whether the transcript is lncRNA. In view of the transcript obtained in the previous step, we synthesized the current mainstream coding potential analysis method(CPC2(Kang YJ et al., 2017)/Pfam(Mistry J et al., 2007)/CNCI(Sun L et al., 2013)) to predict the coding potential, the intersection of transcripts with no coding potential in these software results is taken as the candidate Novel_lncRNA data set predicted by this analysis, the intersection of the transcripts with coding potential in these software results is taken as the Novel_mRNA data set.
step5: According to the naming guidelines of HGNC(The HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee) (Wright MW, 2014) on long non-coding RNA, the Novel_lncRNA candidate was finally screened and named according to its position relationship with coding genes, and the Novel_lncRNA analyzed in this study was obtained.

4.5 Differential Expression Analysis

After the quantitative analysis, the expression matrix of all samples was obtained, and then the significance analysis of expression difference at gene or transcript level could be carried out to find functional genes or transcripts related to the treatment group. we used edgeR(Robinson MD et al., 2010) software to analyze the significance of expression differences. Significance level was determined by pvalue or corrected pvalue (padj). Padj is the value corrected by multiple test of pvalue using BH method when the false positive rate is high. The smaller the corrected pvalue is, the more significant it is. Padj <= 0.05 was used as the difference significance standard (if padj <= 0.05 was too small, pvalue <= 0.05 was used for difference screening, and the specific difference screening conditions were shown in volcano diagram).

The differential analysis is mainly divided into three steps:

(1) the raw readcount is normalized, mainly to correct the sequencing depth;

(2) the statistical model is used to calculate the hypothesis test's probability (pvalue);

(3) multiple hypothesis test corrections are used to obtain FDR values (false discovery rate).

Q:Can I use FPKM for differential analysis?
A:Most of the differential analysis software (DESeq, DESeq2 and edgeR) use the raw read counts as the input file and the negative binomial distribution model to estimate the probability of gene differential expression between samples. The software itself does some corrections on the read count (mainly the depth of sequencing), while FPKM is the corrected expression value, so it is unreasonable to use FPKM for the differential analysis to make double corrections.
Q:Why we use padj, can we use pvalue?
A:Corrected p value (padj/qvalue), is value after applying multiple statistical hypothesis examination on p-value, which is higher than original p-value and more efficient in control false positive.
Q:What is the method for clustering analysis?
A:Clustering uses a build-in R package, pheatmap. We focus on the data (mRNA_Diff_gene_heatmap.xls/lncRNA_Diff_transcript_heatmap.xls), which is the union gene/transcript set of all comparison groups. And we use relative gene/transcript expression level, log2(ratios), to do clustering. The clustering will calculate the distance between each genes/transcripts, and evaluate the relative gene/transcript distance through iteration. Finally, it will divide the gene/transcripts into several subgroups according to gene/transcript distance. H-cluster is the main clustering method used by us.
Q:What is clustering for?
A:Clustering can be used to determine the different gene/transcript expression pattern through gathering the gene/transcript with similar expression. These genes/transcripts with similar expression may have similar function or participate in same pathway and metabolic process. It is possible for us to identify unknown gene/transcript function through clustering. Thus, the clustering is useful in expanding biological understanding of gene/transcript expression.

4.7 Prediction and functional analysis of lncRNA target genes

LncRNA has rich biological functions and is widely involved in various important physiological processes of organisms. It can regulate the expression of target genes at the transcriptional level and post-transcriptional level, and identify the differential functional pathways generated by different treatments (Schmitt AM et al., 2016). LncRNA regulates target genes through various mechanisms, and co-location (also known as Cis) and co-expression (also known as Trans) are the most common ways to regulate target genes. Co-location means that lncRNA may have a regulatory effect on adjacent protein-coding genes. The analysis was conducted by searching for genes within 100KB upstream and downstream of lncRNA (Bao Z et al., 2019). Co-expression refers to the transcriptional action of lncRNA on distant target genes, which is analyzed by expression correlation between multiple samples and generally performed when the sample number is greater than 5 (Kopp F et al., 2018).

we source will conduct target gene prediction for all lncrnas, that is, target genes of lncrnas can be predicted through co-location and co-expression between lncrnas and protein-coding genes. Then, functional enrichment analysis (GO/KEGG) was performed on target genes of differential lncrnas to predict the main functions of lncrnas. Only the prediction results of co-location are shown in the report.

4.8 Alternative Splicing Analysis

4.8.1 Detection of Differentially Expressed Isoforms

Alternative splicing means that the same precursor mRNA has multiple splicing procedures to form different mRNA, which is an important mechanism to regulate gene expression and generate protein diversity. For example, various forms of variable splicing are widely present in human cancer progression, and many cancer-related genes are regulated by variable splicing. Abnormal splicing and changes in the splicing process may have an important impact on tumor genesis and development, and cancer-specific splicing variants have potential diagnostic value, so variable shear analysis has gradually become a hot topic in cancer research in recent years (Scotti MM et al., 2016).

4.9 Mutation

A single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) is a substitution of nucleotide sequences occurring commonly within a population (e.g. 1%), in which a single nucleotide in the genome, or other shared sequence exchanges between members of a biological species or paired chromosomes. Two types of variation occur with SNPs, namely transitions and transversions, occur with a probability ratio of 1:2. The high occurrence region of SNPs is often the CG sequences, resulting in C to T transitions, which are associated with the tendency of C to be methylated. In contrast to SNPs, InDeL is a molecular biology term for an insertion or deletion of bases in the genome of an organism, measuring from 1 to 10 000 base pairs in length, which is classified among small genetic variations.

Mutation site analysis is an important part of RNA-seq structural analysis, including detection of inborn mutation sites and acquired somatic cell mutation sites, which is important for tumor research. We used GATK software(McKenna A et al., 2010) to perform mutation site analysis on sample data and used Snpeff software to annotate the variant sites.

After applying GATK for the detection of variant sites, we counted each variant site according to the SnpEff annotation information mainly including the function statistics of the variant site, the statistics of the variant site and the influence of the variant site. The variant site region (SNP_region) is statistically plotted from gene structure regions such as EXON, INTRON and INTERGENIC. Variant site influence (SNP_impact) is statistically plotted from four levels of HIGH (high), MODERATE (middle), LOW (low), MODIFIER (self-phenotype effect, and other variant sites should have an impact at the same time).
