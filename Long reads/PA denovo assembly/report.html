    1 Summary


    In recent years, with the development of high-throughput sequencing technology, third-generation sequencing has gradually become an emerging tool for genome research. Third-generation sequencing technologies such as Pacbio and Nanopore effectively solve the problem of short read lengths and inability to cross high-repetitive regions of genomes in second-generation sequencing. Currently, Nanopore sequencing technology, as a newcomer in the field of third-generation sequencing, has been widely applied due to its advantages of long read lengths, high output, and low cost. This platform uses nanopores for real-time sequencing, with no read length limitation, and can directly read sequence information of ultra-long fragments, obtaining ultra-long reads. At the same time, with the continuous emergence of new sequencing technologies, third-generation sequencing technologies, combined with new technologies such as Bionano and Hi-C, enable genome assembly to reach the level of Super-Scaffold or even chromosome level.




    2 Background Introduction


      2.1 Pacbio Technical Introduction

    PacBio SMRT technology applies the concept of sequencing during synthesis and uses SMRT chips as sequencing carriers.[1]。

    The basic principle is that DNA polymerase binds to the template and 4-color fluorescently labeled 4 types of nucleotides (i.e., dNTPs). During the base pairing stage, the addition of different nucleotides emits different light, and the type of entering nucleotides can be determined based on the wavelength and peak of the light. At the same time, DNA polymerase is one of the keys to achieving ultra-long read lengths. The read length is mainly related to the activity of the enzyme, which is mainly affected by the damage caused by the laser. One of the keys to PacBio SMRT technology is how to distinguish the reaction signal from the strong fluorescent background of the surrounding free nucleotides. They use the principle of ZMW (zero-mode waveguide) which is similar to the many dense small holes that can be seen on the microwave oven wall. The diameter of the small hole is carefully considered. If the diameter is greater than the microwave wavelength, the energy will penetrate the panel under the effect of diffraction and leak out, thereby interfering with the surrounding small holes. If the aperture is smaller than the wavelength, the energy will not radiate to the surroundings, but will remain in a straight line state (principle of light diffraction), thereby providing protection.

      4.2.1 Hifiasm Genome Assembly

    Hifiasm is a fast de novo assembler designed for PacBio HiFi reads. It can assemble a human genome in a matter of hours and has been used to assemble one of the most complex genomes to date, the California redwood genome. Unlike most existing assembly software, hifiasm calculates from the uncollapsed genome, allowing it to preserve haplotype information as much as possible. Hifiasm supports both primary and full phased assemblies, including trio-binning assembly.

    6 Assembly Result Evaluation


      6.1 Sequence Integrity Assessment

    The so-called integrity assessment refers to evaluating the coverage of the assembled genome to the gene regions. Common methods for genome assembly evaluation include BUSCO evaluation, CEGMA evaluation, EST sequence evaluation, BAC evaluation, and RNA evaluation.

      6.1.1 BUSCO Evaluation

    The BUSCO（Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Orthologs：http://busco.ezlab.org/）evaluation uses a single-copy orthologous gene library and software such as metaeuk and hmmer to assess the assembled genome, thereby evaluating the integrity of the assembled genome.

    According to the statistical results in Table 6-1.1, the genome assembly of XXXXXX has achieved 99.5% completeness of BUSCO genes, indicating good integrity of the assembly result.

      6.2 Sequence Integrity Assessment

    To evaluate the accuracy of the assembly, a small fragment library of reads is selected and aligned to the assembled genome using the BWA software. The alignment rate of the reads, the degree of coverage of the genome, and the distribution of sequencing depth are calculated to assess the completeness of the assembly and the uniformity of sequencing.

    As shown in Table 6-2.1, the small fragment reads were aligned to the genome, with an alignment rate of approximately 99.78% and a genome coverage of approximately 99.91%. This indicates a high level of consistency between the reads and the assembled genome.

    Single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) are genetic markers formed by single nucleotide variations in the genome, with a higher number indicating greater polymorphism. We used tools such as samtools to sort BWA alignment results by chromosome coordinates, remove duplicate reads, and perform SNP calling. We then filtered and analyzed the raw results.

    As shown in Table 6-2.2, the heterozygous SNP rate in the genome is 0.000246%, while the homozygous SNP rate is 2e-06%. It is generally believed that the homozygous SNP rate can reflect the accuracy of genome assembly (the lower the homozygous SNP rate, the higher the accuracy of genome assembly). This result indicates that the assembly has a high single-base accuracy.

      6.3 Sequence Accuracy Assessment

    Merqury is a software tool that evaluates the quality of genomes using Illumina sequencing data based on K-mer analysis, without the need for a reference genome.

    Qv represents the quality value of genome bases. The Qv value of the target genome can be evaluated using the Merqury-mash module with the formula Qv = -10 * logE, where E represents the error rate. When the accuracy is 99%, the Qv value is 20; when the accuracy is 99.9%, the Qv value is 30; and when the accuracy is 99.99%, the Qv value is 40.
