1 Analysis process and method


  1.1 Genome Annotation Analysis Process 

Genome annotation mainly includes three aspects: repetitive sequence annotation, gene annotation (including gene structure prediction and gene function prediction), non-coding RNA (ncRNA) annotation, as shown in Figure 1.1.1：



Figure 1.1 Genome Annotation Flowchart

  1.2 Genome Annotation Analysis Methods 

    1.2.1 Repeat Sequence Annotation Method  

1.2.1.1 Homologous sequence alignment

        The homologous sequence alignment method is based on the repeat sequence database, using Repeatmasker and repeatproteinmask software, to identify sequences similar to known repeat sequences.

1.2.1.2 Ab initio prediction

        Use LTR_FINDER , RepeatScout , RepeatModeler and other software to first establish the De novo repeat sequence library, and then predict it through the Repeatmasker software. In addition, the de novo prediction method can also use TRF to find tandem repeat sequences (Tandem Repeat) in the genome.

    1.2.2 Gene structure prediction methods  

1.2.2.1 Homologous sequence alignment

        The method of homologous prediction is to compare the coding protein sequences of known homologous species with the genome sequences of new species (the number of homologous species is usually 5-6), and use blast, genewise and other comparison software to predict the genetic structure.

1.2.2.2 De novo prediction method

        De novo prediction uses software that relies on the statistical characteristics of genome sequence data (such as codon frequency, exon-intron distribution) to predict gene structure. Commonly used software includes Augustus, GlimmerHMM, SNAP, etc.

1.2.2.3 Other Evidence-Supported Predictive Methods

        Other evidence-supported predictions use EST or cDNA data from homologous species to predict gene structure through blat alignments.

        Based on the above prediction results, combined with the transcriptome comparison data, the EVidenceModeler integration software was used to integrate the gene sets predicted by various methods into a non-redundant and more complete gene set. Finally, using PASA, combined with the transcriptome assembly results, the EVM annotation results were corrected, and information such as UTR and variable splicing was added to obtain the final gene set.


2 Analysis results


  2.1 Repeat sequence annotation


Repeats can be divided into two categories: tandem repeats and interspersed repeats. Tandem repeats include microsatellite sequences, small satellite sequences, etc.; scattered repeats are also called transposon elements, including DNA transposons and retrotransposons that transpose in a DNA-DNA manner. Common types of retrotransposons include LTR, LINE, and SINE. First, integrate the repeated sequence library predicted by Denovo with the homologous repeated sequence database Repbase, and then use the RepeatMasker software to perform repeat annotation on the [xxxx] genome. The results show that the [xxxx] genome contains 53.03% of the repeat sequences. For the statistics of the annotation results, see Tables 2.1.1 and 2.1.2.

  2.4 Noncoding RNA Annotation


       Non-coding RNA refers to RNA that does not translate proteins, such as rRNA, tRNA, snRNA, miRNA, etc. These RNAs have important biological functions. miRNA can degrade its target gene or inhibit the translation of target gene into protein, which has the function of silencing gene. tRNA and rRNA are directly involved in protein synthesis. snRNA is mainly involved in the processing of RNA precursors and is the main component of RNA splicing bodies.
