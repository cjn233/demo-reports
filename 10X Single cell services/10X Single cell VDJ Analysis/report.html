1 Overview

10x Genomics Chromium™ single cell V(D)J product can display adaptive immune responses and immune repertoire information, revealing the diversity of T or B cell populations in humans or mice at the single-cell level. The 10x platform also provides analysis software Cell Ranger and visualization software Loupe V(D)J Browser for analyzing immune products. The overall flowchart of the 10x Genomics Chromium™ single cell V(D)J product is as follows:

First, after the single cell suspension prepared by the customer passes the quality inspection, samples are loaded onto the 10x Genomics Chromium instrument to form GEMs. GEMs are then collected for reverse transcription and cDNA amplification, V(D)J enrichment amplification, library construction, and finally high-throughput sequencing. Finally, the sequencing data is used for bioinformatics analysis to obtain the results of single cell V(D)J analysis.

1.1 Library Construction Process

Novogene constructed and sequenced the library based on the official standards of 10x Genomics. From capturing immune cells to obtaining sequencing data, every step such as sample quality inspection, library construction and sequencing will directly affect the quantity and quality of data, thereby affecting the final results of bioinformatics analysis. To ensure the accuracy and reliability of sequencing data from the source, Novogene promises to strictly control all production processes of the data, ensuring the output of high-quality data from the root. The experimental flowchart of the 10x Genomics Chromium™ single cell V(D)J product is as follows:

The key technology of the 10x Genomics Chromium™ system is to label different samples (long DNA molecules/single cells) with millions of unique barcodes. First, Gel beads containing barcode information are mixed with a mixture of samples and enzymes, and then combined with oil surfactants located in the microfluidic "double cross" crossing system to form GEMs (Gel Bead-In-Emulsions, meaning oil droplets that encapsulate the mixture of Gel beads, samples, and enzymes). Afterwards, GEMs flow into the reservoir and are collected, which is the most critical step. Next, Gel beads dissolve and release the barcode sequence, starting to label the cells. The standard sequencing library is constructed by mixing the products containing barcode information in each droplet. The Gel beads diagram of the 10x Genomics Chromium™ single cell V(D)J product is shown below:

The steps after passing the "double cross" crossing system are as follows:

Step 1: GEMs formation and barcode labeling. The barcode sequence of V(D)J library is connected to the switch oligo TSO sequence. mRNA is first reverse transcribed using ploy (T) primers to the mRNA 5' end, and the sequence CCC is added at the end to complement and pair with the GGG sequence of TSO, achieving cDNA capture. Transcription continues to add barcode sequence to the 5'-end cDNA sequence.

Step 2: V(D)J area enrichment. Nested PCR is performed using universal primers with 5' connector and continuous nested primers in the TCR constant area. The sequencing connector P5 is added in this process to realize V(D)J area enrichment of TCR/BCR.

Step 3: Library construction. The V(D)J cDNA is randomly broken in the way of enzymatic digestion, and the sequence obtained by controlling the broken length can cover the entire V(D)J segment cDNA sequence. The end is repaired and added with A and sequencing connector P7, and PCR is amplified for library construction.

2 Information Analysis Process

The 10x Genomics Chromium™ single cell V(D)J product is specifically enriched with RNA in the area TCR of T cells and the area BCR of B cells. The immune repertoire is constructed through comparison, splicing, screening, annotation, etc., and clonotype typing is performed for obtained TCR/BCR to conduct immunoassay. After obtaining the raw sequenced reads through high-throughput sequencing of TCR/BCR, the main analysis content of single cell V(D)J is as follows:

Part 1: Quality control. The quality control of raw data is carried out through fastp.

Part 2: Analysis of TCR/BCR clone subtypes. Novogene uses the Cell Ranger software provided by 10x Genomics to perform various screening steps, Contig splicing, and sample clonotype typing and abundance analysis.

Part 3: Advanced analysis. In the second part of the analysis, the TCR/BCR clone subtypes of each sample are completely analyzed, and based on this, immunarch and scRepertoire are used to conduct comprehensive analysis of each sample.

3 Quality Control Analysis

3.1 Description of Raw Sequencing Data

The raw image files obtained from high-throughput sequencing (such as Illumina NovaSeq PE150) are converted into sequenced reads through CASAVA base calling and stored in FASTQ format. FASTQ is a common text format for storing biological sequences and corresponding quality values. Taking sequencing data from Illumina NovaSeq PE150 as an example, both ends are 150bp long. The first 28bp of 10X3' transcriptome library read1 is the cell barcode and UMI information (or the first 26bp of 10X5' transcriptome), which is an important basis for distinguishing which cell read2 comes from. The bases after 28bp are non-valid data, while read2 is valid data.

Each read in the fastq format file consists of four lines of descriptive information, as shown below:

@ST-E00310:278:HF3GJALXX:5:1101:6745:1924 1:N:0:ACGCTCGA
TNTAGATCTTAGGCGGTTGGTGTAGGTATCGACACAGGTCTTCATTTTGGCTAATAATGTACCAACGGAAGTCTGACATTCTGACTGTTCTTCTTCCTCTTCTTCCCCATTCTCTGTAGAAAGAGGCAACAACAGCGGCACCATAGATGT
+
F#FFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFF:FFFFF:FFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFF:FFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFF:FFFF:FFFFF:FFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFFF,FFFFFFF

The first line in the above file begins with "@", followed by Illumina sequence identifiers and descriptive text. The second line is the base sequence of the sequencing fragment. The third line begins with a "+" followed by Illumina sequence identifiers (which can also be empty). The fourth line is the sequencing quality value corresponding to each base in the sequencing fragment, and the ASCII value corresponding to each character in this line is subtracted by 33 to obtain the sequencing quality value for that base.

3.2 Quality Evaluation of Sequencing Data

Massive data information can be obtained through sequencing, and how to obtain qualified data from the obtained data is the basis of information analysis. Therefore, quality control (QC) of offline data is the first step in data analysis. fastp is currently a common data quality evaluation software. Basic quality statistics are carried out for raw reads using fastp. In a uniform way, the primary raw reads are named sample name-1,2,3,4... as per the index, lane, or sequencing time. R1 and R2 are read1 and read2 of samples. The data quality will directly affect the results of subsequent information analysis. 4.2 Construction of Immune Repertoire

(1) Assembling contigs: Cellranger filters out barcodes that may contain noise and reads of Unique Molecular Identifiers (UMIs) caused by PCR errors, sequencing errors, etc. Primer sequences at the 5' and 3' ends of reads are removed. These reads are then combined to output a set of assembled contigs, which represent the optimal sequence of the existing transcript sequence. Each base in each contig is assigned a quality value. The number of UMIs and reads supported by each contig is also shown.


(2) Annotating contigs: Contigs are annotated using the V(D)J segment labels and the area CDR3 that forms the transcript is located. Contigs with biological functions are filtered out.


(3) Cell identification: Barcodes/GEMs containing T or B cells are identified.


(4) Generating clonotype: The clonotype of cells is identified

4.4 Clonotypes Statistics

Unique clonotypes are in the system immune system and specifically refer to the unique combination of a specific immune receptor in a group of immune cells (normally lymphocytes, such as T cells and B cells). This unique combination is made up of different parts of immune receptor genes (e.g., segments V, D, J, and C). These parts jointly determine the structure and functions of immune receptors.

In the immune system, each immune cell has a different immune receptor, which is determined by random recombination and variation of genes. Therefore, even if cells are of the same type, their combination of immune receptors may be unique. Each different combination of immune receptors is called a clonotype. Immune cells with the same combination of immune receptors are called members of the same clonotype.

Unique clonotype represents a situation where each clonotype only appears once in a group of immune cells. It is commonly used in immune repertoire analysis and immunology studies to describe the diversity of immune cells and the number of clonotypes. The concept of unique clonotype is important in understanding the immune system function, antibody response, and disease states.

Scale map of unique clonotypes (unique_clonotypes_byaa represents the clonotype with amino acid sequence as unique; sample name on the x-axis, and percentage on the y-axis). Please refer to the result file: /3.Immu_vdj/2.Immu_vdj/*/Unique_clonotypes

Clonotype abundance statistics are used to describe a group of individuals with the same or similar immune receptor sequences (such as immune globulin or T cell receptor). These sequences are produced in the process of genetic recombination and are used to identify and respond to in vivo and exo-antigens.

In the immune system, the immune receptors on the surface of cells are highly diverse because they need to be able to recognize many different antigens. When an antigen matches an immune receptor, the corresponding immune cells (such as B cells or T cells) are activated, triggering an immune response. The diversity of immune receptors is achieved through genetic recombination, which allows immune cells to generate millions or even more different immune receptor variants to adapt to various antigens.

Clonotype abundance statistics involve quantitative analysis of different immune receptor sequences (or clonotypes) present in the sample. It is possible to simultaneously determine a large number of immune receptor sequences in a single sample. This analysis helps to understand how the immune system responds to specific antigens or diseases, as well as the changes in clonotypes under different conditions.

The number of cells with amino acid sequence as the only clonotype and expressing specific clonotypes is the typing abundance. 4.5 CDR3 Length Statistics

CDR3 length distribution statistics are used to determine the frequency distribution of CDR3 area lengths for different immune receptors in a group of samples. This analysis can reveal the changes in CDR3 length in different samples, which helps to understand the diversity of the immune system and the response of immune receptors to specific antigens.

With amino acid sequence as the unique clonotype, the length of clonotypes is shown, and the total length of light and heavy chains is counted. CDR3 consists of a area connected by V and J or D and J, with high variability. CDR3 is the area that determines the clonotype of cells, and in its natural state, the length distribution of CDR3 follows a normal distribution.

Combined with analysis of sequence length and gene segment grouping, more comprehensive understandings of gene distribution, diversity, and clonotype frequency in a sample can be obtained. These understandings are of great significance for exploring gene expression, immune response, and disease-related genes.

4.10 Clonal Diversity Analysis

Different algorithms are adopted to analyze the sample's clonal diversity.

Chao1 index is an ecological indicator to measure species richness. It was first proposed by Chao (1984). The higher the value, the greater the richness of the community species.

InvSimpson, 1-Simpson index value. The larger the InvSimpson index value, the higher the community diversity. (Simpson: One of the indices used to estimate microbial diversity in a sample. The larger the Simpson index value, the lower the community diversity.)

Hill (1973) recognized that species richness, Shannon index, and Simpson index are all members of the same series of diversity indices, and proposed Hill numbers to quantify diversity accordingly. Q in the figure represents the Hill number or effective variety number in the Q stage. When Q=0, the abundance of species is not considered, only the number of species is considered, and the larger the value, the more abundant the species. When Q=1, it is the Shannon index. When Q=2, it is the Simpson index. The slope of the curve reflects the heterogeneity of clonotype abundance, and the more uneven the distribution of clonotype abundance, the sharper the decline of the curve.

Div (effective species count) refers to the number of species in a homogeneous community that is equal to the average proportion of species abundance in a given dataset.

D50, clonotype amino acid sequence is sorted as per the proportion and accumulated from the highest to the lowest. When it reaches 50% of the total sequence, the proportion of CDR3 sequence types is in the curve similar to that of saturation.

5.1 Research Background TCR/BCR

Immune Repertoire (IR) is the sum of diverse T and B cells of all functions in a systemic circulatory system at a given time. V(D)J rearrangement that occurs during the development from hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow to mature T cells in the thymus and mature B cells in the bone marrow can lead to diversity of IR. T cell receptor (TCR) is a molecular structure for T cells to specifically recognize and bind antigen peptide MHC molecules, and its function is to recognize antigens. B cell receptor (BCR) is a membrane surface protein used by B cells to recognize antigens and has antigen binding specificity. After being stimulated by antigens, B cells will proliferate and differentiate into a large number of plasma cells. Plasma cells can synthesize and secrete corresponding immunoglobulins (Igs) for "humoral immunity". There are obvious differences between BCR and TCR structures, as shown in the following figure:

As shown in the figure, BCR is composed of two heavy chains and two light chains connected, where the heavy chains are divided into a variable area (area V), a constant area (area C), a transmembrane area, and a cytoplasmic area, and the light chains only have areas V and C. TCR can be divided into two types, with most T cells having TCR composed of α and β chains, and a few TCR composed of γ and δ peptide chains (Note: The standard reagent protocol on 10x Genomics's official website does not contain γ and δ chain primers.可Please refer to the link on the website.）Each peptide chain of TCR can be divided into area V, area C, transmembrane area, and cytoplasmic area. BCR and TCR have three highly variable areas: CDR1, CDR2, and CDR3. Among them, CDR3 has the largest variation, which determines antigen binding specificity. Studies on the diversity of B cells and T cells are also concentrated in the area CDR3.

As shown in the figure, the area CDR3 of BCR includes light-chain V and J gene segments and heavy-chain V, D and J gene segments, while the area CDR3 of TCR includes α-chain V and J gene segments and β-chain V, D, and J gene segments. Analysis of BCR or TCR based on the differences in CDR3 amino acid sequence can reveal their clonotype typing, V-J gene abundance analysis, and related immune analysis.
