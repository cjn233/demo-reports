1 Introduction

16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is a component of the 30S small subunit of the ribosome in prokaryotic cells, containing 9 hypervariable regions (V1-V9), ranging from about 30-100 base pairs long that are involved in the secondary structure of the small ribosomal subunit. The degree of conservation varies widely between hypervariable regions, with more conserved regions correlating to higher-rank taxonomy and less conserved regions to lower ranks, such as genus and species. Therefore, 16S rDNA can be used in reconstructing bacterial phylogenies and classifications.

Sequence variation in the amplicon gene is widely used to characterize diverse microbial communities. With the continuous development of high-throughput sequencing platforms, the illumina sequencing platform could be used for taxonomic classification. Amplicon sequencing technology gradually becomes an important method to study the composition and structure of the microbial communities.

Additionally, conservative regions enable the design of universal primers. Since 16S rDNA amplicon sequencing typically selects one or several hypervariable regions of interest, the universal primer used during PCR amplification is designed from the conservative regions that encompass the hypervariable regions of interest, which will allow for the identification of microorganisms from the hypervariable regions through subsequent analysis (Caporaso et al., 2011; Youssef et al., 2009; Hess et al., 2011).

In the process from DNA samples to final data, each step such as sample test, PCR, purification, library preparation, and sequencing will affect the quality and quantity of data, while the data quality will directly affect the subsequent information analysis results. In order to keep the accuracy and reliability of sequencing data, quality control (QC) is performed at each step of the procedure.

Sample preparation

Q: Why can the sample be amplified without bands in gel electrophoresis ?

The concentration of the sample is too low to detect the band. PCR is positive because the starting amount of PCR template needs to be very low.

Q: How to detect the concentration of the sample ?

Qubit is used, which can accurately quantify the DNA concentration.

Q: Why does the sample degrade ?

Degradation is caused by too long pyrolysis time and too slow operation. Poor preservation of nucleic acid leads to degradation.

Q: DNA sample purification (RNA digestion) conditions ?

The digestion condition of the experiment is to take stock liquid 3 μL + 6 μL ddH2O + 1 μL RNase A (original concentration 10mg/mL), 37 ℃, 15 min. Then take 2 μL products for electrophoresis.

Q: Impact of too low or too high GC on PCR?

The AT content of DNA is high and the thermodynamic stability is low, which will lead to the inability of polymerase replication or the introduction of errors. High GC content brings high thermal stability. In the process of PCR amplification, high GC templates are difficult to be completely denatured. Even if denatured, when the temperature of the annealing step decreases, these chains may also form strong intramolecular hydrogen bonds, which makes it difficult for the polymerase to play its role.

Q: Which pair of ITS primers is better for distiller's yeast and fermentation samples ?

If the sample type is distiller's yeast or there may be yeast in fermentation, it is recommended to do ITS2. For conventional ITS1, the yeast will be greater than 500 bp; If you do ITS2, double bands may appear on the electrophoretic detection map, and the size of ITS2 fragment is about 430 bp.

Q: Why use 2% gel for PCR product electrophoresis ?

The concentration of agarose gel used in experiments is usually 0.5~2%. The low concentration is used for electrophoresis of large pieces of nucleic acid, and the high concentration is used for small pieces of analysis. What we do is PCR amplification products, and the bands generally do not exceed 500 bp, so we choose high concentration gel for electrophoresis.

Q: The purpose of degenerate bases in primers.

Degenerate base: the nucleotide at a certain position of the designed primer sequence can be two or more different bases. The synthesized primer is a mixture of different sequences at this position, which is intended to increase the amplification efficiency, because research shows that the base site of this segment in different samples is not a fixed conservative sequence.

Negative control

Q: Why there is no negative control set for library building and sequencing of amplicon.

During the construction and sequencing of the amplicon library, the amplification process is all based on the PCR reaction site with a negative control, that is, ddH2O is used as a negative reagent for subsequent PCR operations. Only when the negative control does not have a band, subsequent experiments will be conducted. The reagents used in our primer dilution are all prepared with sterilized ddH2O. Therefore, the use of ddH2O as a negative control in the control can explain the situation, so we are not in the process of negative sample library building, sequencing and other follow-up work. In addition, the experiment uses ddH2O as the negative control. Even if there is slight pollution, the data output rate will be extremely low (that is, CK has no stripes). The number of reads is very small (generally not more than 1000). According to the current information analysis experience, the data of this level is not enough to cause such a high abundance difference in the statistical level in our article results. The high abundance difference in the results should not be caused by reagent contamination.




3 Bioinformatics Analysis Pipeline

The raw data obtained by sequencing has a certain proportion of dirty data. In order to make the results of bioinformatic analysis more accurate and reliable, we do splicing and filtering of raw data to obtain clean data firstly; Based on clean data, we use DADA2 or deblur to reduce noise (DADA2 is used by default) (Li et al., 2020). For the obtained ASVs, on the one hand, the representative sequence of each ASV is annotated to obtain the corresponding species information and the abundance distribution based on the species (Callahan et al., 2017). Then, analyze the abundance, Alpha diversity calculation, Venn graph, and Flower graph of ASVs to obtain the species richness and uniformity information of the samples, and the common and unique ASVs information among different samples or groups. On the other hand, multiple sequence alignment of ASVs can be performed and phylogenetic trees constructed. Through dimensionality reduction analysis such as PCoA, PCA, NMDS, and sample clustering tree display, the differences in community structure between different samples or groups can be explored. In order to further explore the differences in community structure in different groups, statistical analysis methods such as T-test, MetagenomeSeq, LEfSe, etc. are used to test the significance of differences in species composition and community structure of groups. The annotation results of the amplicons can also be associated with the corresponding functional databases, and the PICRUSt2 software can be used to predict and analyze the function of the microbial community in the ecological sample. Flash software merging

Q: What are the conditions for combined reads that have overlap with Flash software? How to deal with those that do not meet the condition? How many bp should be removed from the 3' end and then continue merging?

Flash software has two important parameters in merging: the maximum mismatch rate (0.1) and the minimum overlap region (10bp), that is, we have to ensure that the mismatch rate is no more than 0.1 and the minimum overlap of PE reads is no less than 10 bases when splicing. Considering that the sequence quality at the 3' end has a systematic tendency to decrease, we will partially intercept the PEreads at the 3' end based on the length of the fragment to ensure the length of the overlap region of PE reads, which will help to ensure the quality of the bases in the overlap region and improve the merging rate.

Q: What is the difference between averaging, homogenization and standardization?

Averaging and homogenization both refer to the random sampling of sequences from different samples according to a certain rule (e.g., we go by the minimum number of sequences) so that the number of sequences in different samples is the same. Standardization is generally a Z-value calculation for data that fluctuates over a large range, scaling the data so that it falls into a small specific interval that is easy to display in a graph.



4.2 OTU/ASV analysis and taxonomic annotation

4.2.1 Denoise and Species Annotation

The DADA2 method (Callahan et al., 2016) is mainly used for noise reduction. It no longer uses similarity clustering, but only performs dereplication or equivalent to 100% similarity clustering. Each de-duplicated sequence generated after noise reduction using DADA2 is called ASVs (Amplicon Sequence Variants), or feature sequence (corresponding to the OTU representative sequence), and the abundance table of these sequences in the sample is called the feature table (corresponds to the OTU table). The DADA2 method is more sensitive and specific than the traditional OTU method and can detect the true biological mutations missed by the OTU method while outputting fewer false sequences (Callahan et al., 2019). Compared with OTUs, ASVs improve the accuracy, comprehensiveness, and repeatability of marker gene data analysis (Amir et al., 2017).

By applying QIIME2's classify-sklearn algorithm (Bokulich et al., 2018; Bolyen et al., 2019), a pre-trained Naive Bayes classifier is used for species annotation of each ASV. Annotation database of the project is Silva 138.1.

According to the results of ASVs annotations and the feature tables of each sample, the species abundance tables at the level of kingdom, phyla, class, order, family, genus, and species are obtained. These abundance tables with annotation information are the core content of amplicon analysis. According to different experimental purposes, one or several species of key concern can be selected from the species abundance table of each classification level (usually focusing on the phylum and genus level), combined with the species composition and differential analysis of different samples (groups), and cluster analysis to conduct in-depth research.

Interpretation of archaea results

Q: Why there are many bacteria in the results of archaea annotation ?

The reason is that the content of archaea in the sample is too low. Because of the high homology between archaea and bacteria, the high abundance of bacterial sequences will be amplified during PCR, which is unavoidable. In fact, this is also normal. Just like the endophyte, when the sample is contaminated with plant DNA, 16S primers will amplify the chloroplast sequence (the chloroplast sequence and bacterial sequence are also highly homologous). Therefore, the distribution of sample species directly determines the results of early PCR and late data analysis.

Q: How to understand "relative" in the relative abundance level of bar plot results ?

At a certain classification level (for example, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species), the relative abundance of the species is the number of tags corresponding to a certain level in the sample divided by the total number of tags obtained from the total clustering of the sample. At a taxonomic level, the sum of the relative abundance of all species in the same sample is equal to 1.

Q: Treatment methods for large differences in biological repeated results

We usually recommend more than 5 biological replicates, at least 3. For individual samples with large differences between duplicate samples, it is recommended that:

1) Biological replicates selected from samples may be influenced by many other factors in addition to the set grouping conditions, causing differences in analytical results.

2) For individual samples with significant outliers, it is speculated that the sample may be the cause (e.g., problems during sampling, preservation, extraction, amplification, etc.) and it is recommended that the sample be excluded from analysis.

Q: The union method of "representative sequence" of Venn diagram in groups:

When calculating the number of "representative sequences" in each group, the union method used for counting the representative sequences of the group (that is, if there is only one sample in the group with the representative sequence, it is considered that the representative sequence exists in the group. If there is no representative sequence in all the duplicate samples, it is considered that the representative sequence does not exist in the reorganization).

Q: What is the reason for the heavy host contamination in the amplicon data, and most of them can only be annotated to the Kingdom level after eliminating the hosts ?

The reason is that there are not many sequences left after eliminating the host, and the length of the remaining sequence fragments is short. A short sequence will be annotated to multiple species. When using the lca algorithm to annotate, you will get the annotation results of the previous level, and the results can only be annotated to the limit.

Q: What is the meaning of "others" in the bar plot of Taxa relative abundance?

The Species ranking 11 and later or those not annotated are classified as "others".

Q: What is the reason for some species in the abundance table end with _ or __ , and this species cannot be found in the database ?

If there is an underline at the end of a species name, it is because the species has different evolutionary relationships and some visualizations do not allow duplicate annotation names, so we use an underline to distinguish them. Q: What is the meaning and difference between several Alpha Diversity Indices?

Observed_species index: The number of species (i.e., the number of ASVs) visually observed is counted by randomly sampling a certain amount of sequenced data from the sample. The Rarefaction Curve is a curve constructed from the amount of data extracted and the number of corresponding species. When the curve tends to be flat, it indicates that the amount of sequencing data is progressively reasonable, and more data will only produce a small number of new ASVs.

Goods_coverage index: It is a more commonly used index of sequencing depth, which includes the number of ASVs containing only one sequence and the total number of sequences present in the sample in the calculation, so it can reflect the sequencing depth of the sample more realistically.

Chao1 index: It is one of the widely used Alpha diversity measures in ecology to estimate the total number of species contained in community samples. It also provides a good representation of the presence of low abundance species in the community because it incorporates information on species with abundance of 1 and 2 in its calculation.

ACE index (Abundance Coverage based Estimator): is an index used to estimate the number of ASVs in a community, and is one of the common indices used in ecology to estimate the total number of species. It differs from the Chao1 algorithm in that the number of species with only 1 occurrence, species with 10 or fewer occurrences, and species with more than 10 occurrences are counted separately in its calculation and used as the basis for assessing the number of unmeasured species. ACE takes into account not only the abundance of species but also the probability of species occurrence in the sample, and is therefore a better index of the overall condition of the community anyway.

Shannon diversity index: also known as Shannon-Wiener or Shannon-Weaver index. It is calculated by taking into account the total number of taxa in the sample (Richness), and the proportion of each taxon (Abundance). The higher the community diversity and the more evenly distributed the species, the higher the Shannon index.

Simpson Index: characterizes the diversity and evenness of species distribution within a community by calculating the probability that two randomly sampled individuals belong to different species. Simpson's index can be described at http://www.countrysideinfo.co.uk/simpsons.htm

PD_whole_tree Index: PD index is calculated based on evolutionary distance, and it responds to the kinship of species within the community. The more complex the kinship, the more distant the evolutionary distance, the larger the PD index.

Both shannon and simpson indices are used to describe community diversity (species richness and evenness), while for simpson's index there are three presentations, namely Simpson's Index (D), Simpson's Index of Diversity (1 - D) and Simpson's Reciprocal Index (1 / D). They have similar effects in reflecting community diversity but the results are calculated in different forms. The Simpson's Index of Diversity (1 - D) is the probability that two randomly sampled individuals belong to different species, i.e., the larger the simpson's index, the higher the diversity of the community (the greater the probability that the two sampled individuals belong to different species), so the calculated results and the reflected trend are synchronized with the shannon index. There is also a Simpson's Index (D), which is the probability that two randomly sampled individuals in a community belong to the same species, which is the opposite of the shannon index.

Q: Does the unsaturated ASV Rarefaction curve (not reaching plateau) mean that there is not enough data?

The Rarefaction Curve, which is a random sampling of a certain amount of sequencing data from a sample, counts the number of species they represent (i.e., the number of ASVs). In a dilution curve, when the curve tends to be flat, it indicates that the amount of sequencing data is progressively reasonable, and more data volume will only generate a small number of new OTUs. However, as the amount of sequencing data continues to increase, the curve may have a tendency to increase under relatively relaxed QC conditions, mainly because of sequencing errors and increasing abundance information of sequencing products, causing the curve to maintain an increasing trend.



4.4 Beta Diversity Analysis

Beta Diversity is a comparative analysis of microbial community composition of different samples. First, according to the taxonomy annotation results of all samples and the abundance information of feature sequences, the feature sequence information of the same classification is combined to obtain the species abundance information table (Profiling Table). At the same time, the phylogenetic relationship between feature sequences is used to further calculate the unweighted unifrac distance (Lozupone C et al., 2005; Lozupone C et al., 2011). Unifrac distance is to calculate the distance between samples by using the evolutionary information between microbial sequences in each sample. If there are more than two samples, a distance matrix can be obtained. Then, the weighted unifrac distance (unweighted unifrac) is further constructed using the abundance information of the feature sequence (Lozupone CA et al., 2007). Finally, the differences between different samples (groups) were found through Beta diversity index inter group difference analysis, multivariate statistical methods such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA), Principal coordinates Analysis (PCoA), and Non-Metric Multidimensional Scaling (NMDS).

Then the average distance between the newly created "sample" and other samples is calculated and the two closest samples could be found again to repeat above steps. Q: Interpretation of PCA analysis

In multivariate statistical analysis, principal components analysis (PCA) is a technique for analyzing and simplifying data sets. Principal components analysis is often used to reduce the dimensionality of a dataset while maintaining the features in the dataset that contribute the most to each other's variance. This is done by keeping the lower-order principal components and ignoring the higher-order principal components. The mathematical definition of PCA is an orthogonalized linear transformation that transforms the data into a new coordinate system such that the first major variance of any projection of this data is at the first coordinate (called the first principal component), the second major variance is at the second coordinate (the second principal component), and so on. Given a set of points in Euclidean space, the first principal component corresponds to a line that averages the points through the multidimensional space while ensuring that the sum of squares of the distances from each point to this line is minimized. After removing the first principal component, the second principal component is obtained in the same way. The input data for the PCA plot is the sample microbial species abundance, with the first principal component, i.e., the dimension that contributes most to the difference in species abundance between samples, and the second principal component, i.e., the dimension that contributes second most to the difference in species abundance between samples, in the horizontal coordinate, and the second principal component, i.e., the dimension that contributes second most to the difference in species abundance between samples, in the vertical coordinate. Each sample point represents the projection of the sample point in the multidimensional space of species abundance on the plane of the first and second principal components.

Q: How to get q value by p value correction?

The method is: Benjamini and Hochberg False Discovery Rate. The correction process of this method for p value is as follows:

Here is how it works:

1) The p-values of each gene are ranked from the smallest to the largest.

2) The largest p-value remains as it is.

3) The second largest p-value is multiplied by the total number of genes in gene

list divided by its rank. If less than 0.05, it is significant.

Corrected p-value = p-value*(n/n-1) < 0.05, if so, gene is significant.

4) The third p-value is multiplied as in step 3:

Corrected p-value = p-value*(n/n-2) < 0.05, if so, gene is significant.

And so on.

4.8.3 dbRDA Analysis

The dbRDA analysis (Xiao Qian LI et al., 2015) is mainly used to reflect the relationship between the distance based on flora and environmental factors. RDA analysis reflects the Euclidean distance between quadrats on the graph. The dbRDA analysis is a distance-based redundancy analysis, which is applicable to any distance matrix. 4.8.5 Variance Partitioning Canonical Correspondence Analysis (VPA)

VPA (Variance Partitioning Canonical Correspondence Analysis) (Yang et al., 2007) divides the total variance of the community distribution into the respective contributions and covariations of each set of environmental variables.

The commonly constrained ordinations are RDA and CCA. Constrained ordinations can only demonstrate the variance of the species distribution in response to environmental factors. When the effects of a particular environmental variable on species distribution need to be tested after elimination of possible effects due to other (environmental) variables which are specified as covariables in the constrained analysis, partial ordination may be used (pCCA, pRDA). Furthermore, partial ordination can partition the total variance of the species table into the respective contribution of each set of environmental variables and their covariations using both standard and partially constrained ordinations.
